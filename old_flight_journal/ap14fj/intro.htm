The mission

Although Apollo 14 achieved only the third successful landing on the Moon, it was already the sixth successful flight to the Moon. NASA had made flying to the Moon look easy, almost routine. Even the near disaster of Apollo 13 just 9 months previously had been handled so well that it only served to further convince people that NASA had everything under control. For the general public, flying to the Moon had become routine, almost boring. However, as the readers of this journal know, there was nothing routine about flying to the Moon, especially for the astronauts doing the flying.

Apollo 14 was scheduled to be the last of the "engineering" flights, designed to further check out the complex equipment and procedures needed to land on the Moon. It was essential that the flight go well to clear the way for the more demanding, more exploration oriented flights to come.

About the journal

Shortly after the flight of Apollo 14, NASA prepared a series of transcripts which meticulously documented what was said by the astronauts and some of the ground support people during the flight. The following is an annotated combination of the transcripts which provide the reader with a very detailed look at the flight of Apollo 14. The bulk of the journal comes from the transcript of the radio traffic between the astronauts and Mission Control Center. Here you see NASA at its professional best, working through the mission and any problems that arise. These entries all start with the GET, or time since the start of the mission, in hours, minutes and seconds, at which the text was spoken. This is followed by the name of the speaker, usually one of the astronauts or the current Capcom. This is followed by the text that was spoken, complete with the misspeaks, pauses, and other extras that make up human speech. The following is a list of the major speakers:

The astronauts
Mission commander Alan B. (Al) Shepard, Jr.
Command module pilot Stuart A. (Stu) Roosa
Lunar module pilot Edgar D. (Ed) Mitchell
The Capcoms
Apollo 13 astronaut Fred Haise
Astronaut Gordon (Gordo) Fullerton
Astronaut Bruce McCandless

   From the very beginning of the space program, NASA realized that the general public would have trouble understanding the very jargon heavy radio traffic. So they provided a Public Affairs Officer whose job it was to provide an easy to understand commentary about what was being said and how the mission was progressing. This commentary continues to serve this purpose today and has been included as the dark green text, starting with "Public Affairs Officer".

The last transcript making up the journal is perhaps the most interesting, or at least the most revealing. It comes from the onboard tape recording system whose contents could be send back to Earth over the telemetry channel. What makes this onboard text so interesting is not that it comes from what the press at the time despised as the "secret" radio channel which allowed the astronauts to talk privately with Mission Control. In fact, the onboard system was used almost exclusively as a cockpit voice recorder. It recorded what was said in the spacecraft during critical phases of the mission such as major course corrections. Much of this is only the routine reading of check lists. However, it also provides a look at what the astronauts were saying when they were free from the rigors of radio discipline. Surprisingly often they show themselves to be almost overwhelmed by the beauty of what they were seeing or thrilled at actually being on the way to the Moon. At other times, the enormous demands of trying to do a very complicated job as coolly and professionally as possible got to be too much for them. They needed to fly a nominal mission to show that NASA had recovered from the near disaster of Apollo 13 and was ready for more advanced missions. Any serious error on their part or major technical problem would not only endanger their mission, their lives, but also the rest of the Moon program. It is quite understandable that they would occasionally respond in a very human manner. For the astronauts, there was never anything "routine" about flying to the Moon.
   Like the radio traffic, the onboard text includes a GET and the name of the speaker. To set it apart from the radio traffic, it is colored dark red and contains (onboard) after the speaker.

A large amount of annotation has also been added to make the transcripts easier to understand, to provide some addition, hopefully interesting information, and to simply provide an outlet for at least some of the vast amount of Apollo trivia that we have collected over the years. These annotations are colored dark blue.

The making of this journal

This is a work in progress, aiming to provide interested readers with an in depth look at the flight of Apollo 14. Feedback from readers is desired. Although great care was taken in preparing this journal, we have probably committed a fair number of errors. Even the original transcripts, meticulously prepared by paid professionals, contained some errors. Many of these errors were only revealed when we attempted to merge the various transcripts into one document. We only attempted to correct errors that probably occurred in the original transcription process. Other than this, no changes were made to the original text. For interested readers, the original documents are available as PDF files at several web sites including:
